Jan 26th BLOG Post 1

Used mostly as a social media for the common man TikTok has begun to add corporations and politicians to its platform. The application started off as a sort of revamped karaoke machine, encouraging informality and the combination of song and video. Not the sort of place you would find organizations who have traditionally tried to maintain a formal presence. So you can see why seeing a major organizations mascot twerking dressed as Shrek has made people pause. The motivations for such an act I would argue, seem to be deeply rooted in various forms of convergence that have garnered both respect and trepidation to the company and changed the culture of professional relationships between the public and companies.

In his book Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide, Henry Jenkins says talking about our society, “In this emerging media system, what might traditionally be understood as media producers and consumers are transformed into participants who are expected to interact with each other according to a new set of rules which none of us fully understands”. The hallmark of this quote is that these new rules are quite immaterial (and I would argue that it’s because that they are still being written) so I can’t go into what rule this TikTok is establishing, but I can talk about the ones it’s breaking.

Starting from before TikTok, companies are a multi personal conglomerate, so its representation has been one of cool professionalism. Certainly the biggest scandals in business has been when one person has take to representing the company in a manner not befitting the ideals and the true values of everyone in the company and society at large. Leading to their quick expulsion and an apology generated by a new united front of the company.

The public on the other hand finds it hard to relate to business who talk to them behind their cool grey walls. Like a bad long distance relationship it’s just hard to relate to someone who is interacting with you in clipped words and through a medium. So it's little wonder that they go and cheat on them all the time. Can’t be loyal (or in a healthy relationship ) with something that feels likes ChatBot Ai.

Interestingly enough with the emergence of social media there has been a cultural shift in these “talks over the wall”. Companies found customers to be more loyal when they respond like an actual person. Social media became the window into which the company could show case their humanity and interact with the public sphere. No longer are organizations untouchable entities, with social media you can DM Corporate Disney, and tweet at Wendy’s and get a response. Then came TikTok.

Young millennials and the growing generation of Gen Z began collaborating and colonizing the app like mold on an unchecked sandwich in the back of the fridge. They started creating a place for popular culture to be created and destroyed in witty comments and video recreations. While not always so dingy, it has become quite niche. Virality becomes a competition in the strange and robust. So when the corporate form descended on TikTok they knew they needed to adjust or they weren’t going to survive.

This leads us to the Duolingo mascot twerking. In its history Duolingo has been on the forefront of this cultural convergence. This video is one of many, that pick up on the popular hashtags and viral content being created within and without the application, and recreating it. They generate content that seems a little too tongue-in-cheek for something that was a”cold walled” organization. Seems weird that suddenly this nonhuman corporation is generating content comparable to tween girls, but the reaction has been news-worthy. I have even started following them on the platform, because simply the cold culture is no more. It’s been combined into the public sphere of instant access and companies have forgone the walls and instead have captured the eye of the people with their humanity.

In my analysis the culture shift has been one of separation to a literal overlay of the two spheres together. The publics have turned to organizations for content like they turn to their own friends, not for press releases but for popular culture commentary. With technology you can now twerk along with the best, including your new friends at Duolingo. Informality has won over the cultural divide, and humanity has been taking to the big brick buildings on the hill.

Mar 16th BLOG Post 2

I was recently watching an animated classic with a group composed of my roommates, and neighbors. The story is about the missing Russian princess and the tale of her discovery. Within the film, you interact with many characters, with one thing in common. They are all white. You don’t notice it really, at least I didn’t until they get to Paris. The clip attached shows the whole song but the most impressionable part starts 13 seconds in. Here is where we see a darker-skinned character in a banana skirt walking a wild cat (maybe a Cheetah based on spot shape). In terms of uniqueness, this character stands out and that seems to be the point. She feels novel and exotic sporting tropical fruit and a wild animal in the streets of Paris. Even for us watching, we notice it and feel it like it’s a circus show on the streets. This plays into the novelties of race, the danger (symbolic in the Cheetah), and the foreign nature of those who come from places different than us.

This movie was released in 1997 and you could argue we have come a long way when it comes to Hollywood and whitewashing media, but we still do this. Gods of Egypt a movie released in 2016 was criticized for the same thing. Even Bullet Train released in 2022, set in Tokyo was notably lacking. “Unless you saw the individual character posters, you’d be unlikely to think Bullet Train actually stars any Asian talent” (Francisco, 2022). Bullet Train’s problem adds more to our conversation than Gods of Egypt does because Bullet Train had multiple racial actors involved in the main cast. It shares with our cat-walking character that their race and culture have been white-washed and served on a palatable platter. Bullet Train played to stereotypes of Japanese Anime and Anastasia did the same. It made the two Japanese actors and black circus characters trivial and almost magical through the simplification of cultural iconography. In our modern world, the representation often looks like Bullet Train. Taking the culture of different races and then shelling the race part out of it. Taking the shell and making it more tolerable for white audiences.

This has been happening for centuries, from Minstrel shows of the pre-Jim Crow era to now with hype beasts collecting street culture. Capitalism has taken what identities of other races, and their culture, and turned them into profit because of public demand. Supreme and other big brands built their entire empire on revamping street style that has deep roots in the Harlem Renaissance. We represent race in the same ways by building movies and characters upon the opportunity to use their culture for comedy, mysticism, and elevation. Almost like a spice cabinet, the media has been using races as a flavor to bring some depth, but it often misses the mark. “Whitewashing” as it were is frankly irresponsible. Identity isn’t something you can candy-pick and maximize. Race and culture should be interconnected responsibly and with respect. Culture is not always tied to race, but ethnicity isn’t something you get a free pass to use.

In the end, the media has made a game of using ethnicity for intrigue and applying it to the “woke” crowd. In their attempts to generate new content, they have continued the age-old tradition of pandering culture like a traveling circus. It's what has worked in the past, it worked for Anastasia. It made the city of Paris seem to be the wild exotic place they wanted it to be. The Bullet Train had the excitement of foreign danger, with ninjas and anime cats. The problem is that when you take people out of the culture like Bullet train it feels like appropriation. No matter who is in the cast. If you push character stereotypes like in Anastasia, you create hierarchy and continue the idea that other cultures are simple facades and playthings you can order from a tent. Representation in media it seems can’t win with the ethics we have today.

The problems that we are facing today have the same roots as they did back in 1997 because we haven’t solved those. The media reflects the bigger fault of our society and the demand for entertainment must be reflective to be relevant. It turns to us as people to demand more from entertainment like Newton Minow and find the Golden mean in our representation of each other to get to a place of true equality. To survive the woke generation media must make sure what they are doing isn’t sterilizing other people and their identities, but also showcasing each other’s identities as things that are substantial not a roadshow. This will require a deep journey into the space between the two options and for people to be active in the conversation.

In the future and going forward media should not create one character that fulfills stereotypes but multiple that challenges bias. That is the closest to the Golden Mean that I can see. It then becomes the media’s job to impress the people into tackling the issues that society needs to overcome. We need to rethink the importance of the role of race and the representation of ethnicity as more than just toys. People need to be people, and culture is important to them. Things won't work if we keep that out of our minds.

Francisco, E. (2022, August 6). 'Bullet train' trailers prove Hollywood still hasn't learned one crucial lesson. Inverse. Retrieved March 15, 2023, from https://www.inverse.com/entertainment/bullet-train-asian-representation-andrew-koji-hiroyuki-sanada
