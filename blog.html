Jan 26th BLOG Post 1

Used mostly as a social media for the common man TikTok has begun to add corporations and politicians to its platform. The application started off as a sort of revamped karaoke machine, encouraging informality and the combination of song and video. Not the sort of place you would find organizations who have traditionally tried to maintain a formal presence. So you can see why seeing a major organizations mascot twerking dressed as Shrek has made people pause. The motivations for such an act I would argue, seem to be deeply rooted in various forms of convergence that have garnered both respect and trepidation to the company and changed the culture of professional relationships between the public and companies.

In his book Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide, Henry Jenkins says talking about our society, “In this emerging media system, what might traditionally be understood as media producers and consumers are transformed into participants who are expected to interact with each other according to a new set of rules which none of us fully understands”. The hallmark of this quote is that these new rules are quite immaterial (and I would argue that it’s because that they are still being written) so I can’t go into what rule this TikTok is establishing, but I can talk about the ones it’s breaking.

Starting from before TikTok, companies are a multi personal conglomerate, so its representation has been one of cool professionalism. Certainly the biggest scandals in business has been when one person has take to representing the company in a manner not befitting the ideals and the true values of everyone in the company and society at large. Leading to their quick expulsion and an apology generated by a new united front of the company.

The public on the other hand finds it hard to relate to business who talk to them behind their cool grey walls. Like a bad long distance relationship it’s just hard to relate to someone who is interacting with you in clipped words and through a medium. So it's little wonder that they go and cheat on them all the time. Can’t be loyal (or in a healthy relationship ) with something that feels likes ChatBot Ai.

Interestingly enough with the emergence of social media there has been a cultural shift in these “talks over the wall”. Companies found customers to be more loyal when they respond like an actual person. Social media became the window into which the company could show case their humanity and interact with the public sphere. No longer are organizations untouchable entities, with social media you can DM Corporate Disney, and tweet at Wendy’s and get a response. Then came TikTok.

Young millennials and the growing generation of Gen Z began collaborating and colonizing the app like mold on an unchecked sandwich in the back of the fridge. They started creating a place for popular culture to be created and destroyed in witty comments and video recreations. While not always so dingy, it has become quite niche. Virality becomes a competition in the strange and robust. So when the corporate form descended on TikTok they knew they needed to adjust or they weren’t going to survive.

This leads us to the Duolingo mascot twerking. In its history Duolingo has been on the forefront of this cultural convergence. This video is one of many, that pick up on the popular hashtags and viral content being created within and without the application, and recreating it. They generate content that seems a little too tongue-in-cheek for something that was a”cold walled” organization. Seems weird that suddenly this nonhuman corporation is generating content comparable to tween girls, but the reaction has been news-worthy. I have even started following them on the platform, because simply the cold culture is no more. It’s been combined into the public sphere of instant access and companies have forgone the walls and instead have captured the eye of the people with their humanity.

In my analysis the culture shift has been one of separation to a literal overlay of the two spheres together. The publics have turned to organizations for content like they turn to their own friends, not for press releases but for popular culture commentary. With technology you can now twerk along with the best, including your new friends at Duolingo. Informality has won over the cultural divide, and humanity has been taking to the big brick buildings on the hill.
